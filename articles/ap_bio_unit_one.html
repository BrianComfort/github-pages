The properties of Water

Water has many unique properties that give reason to why it is the most crucial element to sustain life anywhere on Earth.

  • Cohesion
  • Surface Tension
  • High Heat Vaporization
  • Universal Solvent
  • Adhesion

Cohesion

Cohesion is the property of water that describes water "sticking" to itself. A very common example is using a pippet and dropping water droplets onto the face of a penny.

Surface-Tension

Similar to cohesion, surface tension describes the tendency water has to stick together. For example, firetrucks use soap in their water in order to prevent this tendency to better put out fires.

High-Heat Vaporization

Water is very resistant to temperature change/a lot of energy is required for a phase change. Think of specific heat capacity. An example is boiling water takes a while.

Another important thing to understand is when water freezes, the warmest part is right underneath the ice. It's necessary to understand for future lessons/AP test. The example is ice but it can also be said for why we sweat. Sweat allows for evaporation/giving off heat to the environment. The coolest part of the reaction is right underneath the sweat at the level of the skin as energy/heat is being transferred to the water and then to the water vapor in the air.

Universal Solvent

Probably yhe mosts known property is water's ability to dissolve many things. The reason behind this is water's polarity and cohesion properties. As shown in the diagram below, water will "steal" elections from moulcues and bind them. You can see this snow ball effect.

Adhesion

(tk). Adhesion is similar to cohesion but rather describes water's tendency to "stick" to other things other than water. Capillary action is also a part of this.

An example of adhesion in the real world is how trees are capable of bringing water up their roots into their leaves.

Water as a Solid

The reason behind why water is less dense as a solid is for two reasons:

  1. Air bubbles add to water's frozen bouncy
  2. Water formas a hexagonal crystal when combined, allowing for greater "room" inside molecules.
